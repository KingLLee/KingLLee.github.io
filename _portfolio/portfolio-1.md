### Motivation
The El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is one of the most significant interannual oscillations with coupled atmosphere-ocean processes in the tropical Pacific. Most coupled climate models are weak in depicting ENSO asymmetry over the equatorial Pacific subsurface, and it remains unclear how stand-alone ocean models contribute to this bias.

### Approach
- Analyzed OMIP2 multi-model simulations and ocean reanalysis datasets to diagnose subsurface temperature response asymmetry
- Designed dynamical diagnostics linking zonal current variability to thermocline tilt and temperature response asymmetry
- Applied composite analysis to separate responses to westerly and easterly wind stress anomalies

### Key Findings
- Ocean models systematically underestimate the asymmetric temperature response to zonal wind-stress forcing in the western Pacific
- A generally weaker zonal current response in ocean models—linked to a less steep zonal tilt of the thermocline—is the primary cause of the underestimated asymmetry
- Weaker zonal currents lead to biased vertical motion responses, which in turn cause weaker asymmetric temperature responses through dynamic heating
- The relationship between asymmetry and nonlinear dynamic heating in the western Pacific subsurface is opposite to that in the eastern Pacific
